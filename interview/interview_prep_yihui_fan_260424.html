Must Ask
Question

Follow-ups (if they mention A/B test)
  1. What's your hypothesis for the A/B test?
  2. How would you decide how long to run the test?
  3. At the end of the test, 2 of the 10 metrics you monitored are statistically significant, but significant in the wrong direction. What recommendation would you give to the PM?
  1. A/B 테스트의 가설은 무엇인가요?
  2. 테스트 기간을 어떻게 결정하시겠어요?
  3. 테스트 종료 시, 모니터링한 10개 지표 중 2개가 통계적으로 유의미하지만 반대 방향입니다. PM에게 어떤 권고를 하시겠어요?
Expected Strong Answers
Sub-questionWhat to listen for
Approach Structured framework: define success metric(s), design A/B test, determine sample size, run test, analyse results, make recommendation. Should mention guardrail metrics.
Hypothesis Clear null/alternative: H0 = no difference in engagement metric; H1 = treatment increases engagement. Should specify the primary metric (e.g., DAU, session time, retention).
Test duration Power analysis discussion: desired significance level (alpha), power (1-beta), MDE, estimate of variance. Should mention practical considerations (day-of-week effects, novelty effects, network effects).
2 of 10 wrong direction Multiple comparisons / multiple testing problem. Should mention Bonferroni correction, FDR (Benjamini-Hochberg), or similar. Should reason about whether the 2 metrics are primary vs. secondary, effect sizes, and whether "wrong direction" results are practically significant. Mature answer: contextualise the trade-off rather than blindly reject.

Analytical Communication — Framing Results for Customers

10 min
Must Ask
Redesigned from basic descriptive stats (too easy for this candidate) to test the actual manager skill: translating ambiguous analysis into decisions that move customers. A strong IC could ace the old questions; only a senior DS manager can answer these well.
Questions
Q1 — Reframing inconvenient findings. Your analysis shows that a client's current campaign structure is suboptimal — too many micro-budgeted campaigns fragmenting the learning signal. The client built this structure themselves and is emotionally invested in it. How do you present that finding? Walk me through the narrative you'd construct.
▸ If they need context — example scenario
The client runs 18 separate campaigns for one app, each at $30/day. Your analysis shows the ML model needs at least $100/day per campaign to accumulate enough conversion signal to optimize. They built this structure because their previous DSP let them A/B test creatives at campaign level — so there's a logic to it. How do you open the conversation without triggering defensiveness?
Q2 — Holding your ground under pushback. A client pushes back on your recommendation, citing one counter-metric that contradicts your conclusion. How do you decide in the room whether to concede, hold your position, or defer to more data?
▸ If they need context — example scenario
You recommend consolidating from 18 campaigns to 5 higher-budget ones. The client says: "But Campaign #12 has our best CPI at $1.20 — why would we cut it?" Campaign #12 has spent $90 in total over 3 months on 4 conversions. How do you respond in the room without dismissing their concern or caving to a statistically meaningless data point?
Q3 — Communicating ambiguity. Your analysis has three plausible interpretations and the data can't cleanly distinguish between them. How do you communicate that uncertainty to a non-technical account lead without losing credibility or the room?
▸ If they need context — example scenario
A client's install volume dropped 40% last week. Three equally plausible causes: (a) seasonal demand drop in the gaming vertical, (b) top creative rotated out and replacements haven't caught up yet, (c) a Moloco model update shifted bidding patterns. You don't yet have enough data to confirm which. The account lead has a client call in 2 hours. What do you tell them to say?
Q4 — Outlier treatment as a stakeholder decision. You're analysing user purchase data and notice a few extreme high-value outliers that significantly affect the mean CPI. How do you decide which outlier treatment to apply — and how do you explain that choice to a client whose data it is?
▸ If they need context — example scenario
A single whale user made a $10,000 in-app purchase in one day, inflating average revenue per install by 35%. You need to report ROAS to the client. Include the outlier and ROAS looks strong; cap at the 99th percentile and it drops meaningfully. Which do you choose — and what do you tell the client about why you made that call with their data?

Q2 — 반박 속에서 입장 유지. 고객이 귀하의 권고안에 반박하며 귀하의 결론과 모순되는 지표 하나를 제시합니다. 그 자리에서 양보할지, 입장을 유지할지, 더 많은 데이터를 기다릴지 어떻게 결정하시나요?

18개 캠페인을 5개의 고예산 캠페인으로 통합하기를 권고했습니다. 고객이 말합니다: "하지만 캠페인 #12의 CPI가 $1.20으로 최고인데 — 왜 그것을 없애야 하나요?" 캠페인 #12는 3개월 동안 4건의 전환으로 총 $90을 지출했습니다. 통계적으로 의미 없는 데이터 포인트에 굴복하거나 고객의 우려를 무시하지 않고 그 자리에서 어떻게 대응하시겠어요?

Stats/Prob — A/B Test: Understanding Test

10 min
Must Ask
Questions
  1. Walk me through an A/B test you've designed and shipped. What was the hypothesis, and how did you decide it was successful?
  2. What are common ways A/B tests go wrong, and how would you detect or prevent them?
  3. If an A/B test shows no statistically significant difference, how do you decide what to do next?
  1. 직접 설계하고 출시한 A/B 테스트를 설명해주세요. 가설은 무엇이었고, 성공 여부를 어떻게 결정했나요?
  2. A/B 테스트가 잘못되는 흔한 원인은 무엇이고, 어떻게 감지하거나 예방하시나요?
  3. A/B 테스트에서 통계적으로 유의미한 차이가 없을 때, 다음에 무엇을 해야 할지 어떻게 결정하시나요?
Expected Strong Answers
Sub-questionWhat to listen for
Q1: Past A/B test Clear STAR-format story covering: business context, hypothesis formulation, metric selection, sample size/duration, randomisation unit, results interpretation, launch decision. For a manager: evidence of guiding a team through this process, not just individual execution.
Q2: Ways tests go wrong Sample ratio mismatch (SRM), novelty/primacy effects, interference/network effects, instrumentation bugs, peeking (early stopping without correction), Simpson's paradox, survivorship bias, leaky experiments. Detection: SRM checks, A/A tests, pre-experiment balance checks, sequential testing frameworks.
Q3: Non-significant result Distinguish "no effect" from "underpowered test." Check power retrospectively. Consider: increase sample size, extend duration, refine the treatment, look at subgroup analyses (with caution), examine CI width. Decision framework: if CI rules out meaningful effect sizes → confident in no effect; if CI is wide → inconclusive.
Follow-up Probes — Dig Deeper
  • "You said the test was successful — how did you distinguish 'successful' from just 'statistically significant'? What was your actual decision threshold?"
  • "Which of those failure modes have you personally caught in the wild? Walk me through one specific example."
  • "Your CI is wide and the result is non-significant. The PM is waiting for a decision right now. What do you actually tell them?"

Walk me through an A/B test you've designed and shipped. What was the hypothesis, and how did you decide it was successful?직접 설계하고 출시한 A/B 테스트를 하나 설명해 주세요. 가설은 무엇이었고, 성공 여부를 어떻게 결정했나요?
Y
One I'd use as a concrete example: we were testing whether shifting budget allocation from last-click attributed channels toward modelled incrementality signals would improve LTV per dollar spent. The hypothesis was that channels with high last-click attribution were capturing credit for organic intent rather than generating it — so reallocating away from them would reduce spend without reducing installs, and the LTV of retained installs would be higher.

We ran a geo-holdout test — split markets into test and control, applied the new allocation model to test markets, held control on the existing attribution-driven approach. Duration was 8 weeks to capture a meaningful LTV signal beyond day-7. Success criterion was pre-specified: incremental revenue per dollar in test markets above 1.0x versus control, at 80% power.

The test came back positive. But the part I emphasise to my team: we called it successful not because p < 0.05, but because the effect size was above our pre-specified MDE and the confidence interval excluded zero with margin. There's a difference between "statistically significant" and "confident enough to bet $50M of budget on."구체적인 예로 들 수 있는 것: 라스트 클릭 기여 채널 중심에서 모델링된 증분성 신호 기반으로 예산 배분을 전환했을 때 달러당 LTV가 개선되는지 테스트했습니다. 가설은 라스트 클릭 기여율이 높은 채널이 유기적 의향의 공을 가져가고 있지 실제로 만들어내는 게 아니라는 것이었습니다 — 그래서 그쪽 채널에서 예산을 빼도 인스톨이 줄지 않고, 남은 인스톨의 LTV는 더 높을 것이라고 봤습니다.

지역 홀드아웃 테스트로 진행했습니다 — 시장을 테스트/대조군으로 분리하고, 새로운 배분 모델을 테스트 시장에 적용, 대조군은 기존 기여 기반 방식 유지. 기간은 7일 이상의 LTV 신호를 포착하기 위해 8주. 성공 기준은 사전에 명시했습니다: 대조군 대비 테스트 시장의 달러당 증분 매출 1.0x 이상, 검정력 80%.

테스트는 긍정적으로 나왔습니다. 하지만 제가 팀에게 강조하는 부분: p < 0.05이기 때문이 아니라, 효과 크기가 사전에 정한 MDE 이상이고 신뢰구간이 충분한 여유로 0을 제외했기 때문에 성공이라고 판단했습니다. "통계적으로 유의미하다"와 "5천만 달러 예산을 걸 만큼 확신한다" 사이에는 차이가 있습니다.
⚑ Strong Hire — clear STAR format, pre-specified decision threshold (not just p<0.05), and the distinction between statistical significance and decision confidence is the mark of a senior practitioner. For a manager: framed as something she taught her team, not just executed herself.⚑ Strong Hire — 명확한 STAR 구조, 사전 명시된 성공 기준(p<0.05가 아닌), 통계적 유의미성과 의사결정 신뢰도의 구분은 시니어 실무자의 표지. 매니저 관점: 개인 실행이 아닌 팀에게 가르친 것으로 프레이밍.
H
How did you decide the test was successful versus just statistically significant? What was your actual decision threshold?테스트가 성공적이었다고 했는데 — "성공"과 "통계적으로 유의미함"을 어떻게 구분하셨나요? 실제 의사결정 임계값은 무엇이었나요?
Y
We set the MDE upfront based on what the business needed — in that case, a 5% improvement in incremental revenue per dollar was the minimum that would justify the operational cost of switching allocation models. Anything below that, even if significant, wouldn't clear the implementation bar.

So success meant: statistically significant AND effect size above 5% AND the CI lower bound still positive. Not any one of those — all three. That framing matters because a test can be significant with a 1% effect at high power, or it can be non-significant with a 6% effect at low power — those are completely different decisions and need to be treated differently.사업 요건에 기반해 MDE를 사전에 정했습니다 — 그 경우에는 달러당 증분 매출 5% 향상이 배분 모델 전환의 운영 비용을 정당화하는 최솟값이었습니다. 그 이하라면 유의미해도 실행 기준을 통과하지 못합니다.

성공이란 이 세 조건을 모두 충족하는 것이었습니다: 통계적 유의미성 AND 효과 크기 5% 이상 AND 신뢰구간 하한이 여전히 양수. 셋 중 하나만이 아닌 모두. 이 구분이 중요한 이유 — 높은 검정력에서 1% 효과로 유의미할 수도 있고, 낮은 검정력에서 6% 효과로 비유의미할 수도 있는데, 이 둘은 완전히 다른 의사결정이기 때문입니다.
⚑ Strong — MDE anchored to business cost, three-condition success criterion. A Hire answer sets alpha and power but doesn't connect MDE to a business threshold.⚑ 강함 — 사업 비용에 고정된 사전 MDE, 세 조건 성공 기준. Hire 수준 답변은 alpha와 검정력은 설정하지만 MDE를 사업 임계값과 연결하지 않습니다.
H
What are common ways A/B tests go wrong, and how would you detect or prevent them?A/B 테스트가 잘못되는 흔한 원인은 무엇이고, 어떻게 감지하거나 예방하시나요?
Y
The ones I've seen most often in practice: sample ratio mismatch — your treatment and control groups end up with unequal sample sizes, which usually means the randomisation is broken or there's a logging bug. You catch it by checking SRM on day one, not at the end. Novelty effects — users engage with something just because it's new, not because it's better; you need enough runtime to see past the novelty curve. Peeking — teams check results early and stop when they see significance, which inflates the false positive rate; sequential testing frameworks or pre-committed end dates solve this. And leaky experiments — treatment users interact with control users in ways that dilute the effect, which is especially common in social or multiplayer games where network effects are real.

On the prevention side: A/A tests before you run anything important, pre-experiment balance checks on the outcome variable, and a clear experiment review process before launch — not just technical checks, but someone asking "is the randomisation unit right for this hypothesis?"실무에서 가장 많이 본 것들: 샘플 비율 불일치(SRM) — 처치군과 대조군 샘플 크기가 달라지는 것으로, 보통 무작위 배정이 깨지거나 로깅 버그가 있다는 의미입니다. 종료 시점이 아닌 첫날 SRM을 확인해서 잡습니다. 신기 효과 — 사용자가 더 좋아서가 아니라 새롭기 때문에 반응하는 것; 신기 효과 곡선을 지나기에 충분한 운영 기간이 필요합니다. 피킹 — 팀이 중간에 유의미한 결과를 보고 조기 종료하는 것, 이는 거짓 양성율을 높입니다; 순차 검정 프레임워크나 사전 약속된 종료일이 해결합니다. 그리고 누수 실험 — 처치군 사용자가 대조군 사용자와 상호작용해 효과가 희석되는 것, 특히 네트워크 효과가 있는 소셜이나 멀티플레이어 게임에서 흔합니다.

예방 측면에서: 중요한 테스트 전 A/A 테스트, 결과 변수의 사전 실험 균형 확인, 그리고 명확한 실험 검토 프로세스 — 기술적 검토만이 아니라 "이 가설에 무작위 배정 단위가 맞는가?"를 묻는 사람이 있어야 합니다.
⚑ Strong Hire — SRM + novelty + peeking + network effects with detection methods. "Is the randomisation unit right for this hypothesis?" is the bar-raiser signal.⚑ Strong Hire — SRM + 신기 효과 + 피킹 + 네트워크 효과, 감지 방법 포함. "이 가설에 무작위 배정 단위가 맞는가?"라는 질문이 바 레이저 신호.

Y
SRM, multiple times. The most memorable: we ran a monetisation feature test and the SRM check flagged that treatment had 15% fewer users than expected. The team wanted to proceed anyway because the directional signal was positive. I stopped it — a 15% SRM means you don't know which users you're actually measuring. We dug in and found the assignment logic was only firing for users who'd already opened the store tab at least once. So we were measuring engaged buyers, not the full population — a completely different group from what the test was designed around. The positive signal was selection bias, not treatment effect. We fixed the assignment logic and re-ran. H
If an A/B test shows no statistically significant difference, how do you decide what to do next?A/B 테스트에서 통계적으로 유의미한 차이가 없을 때, 다음에 무엇을 해야 할지 어떻게 결정하시나요?
Y
First thing I'd do is look at the CI, not just the p-value. A non-significant result with a narrow CI that excludes meaningful effect sizes is actually informative — you can say with reasonable confidence the effect is small. A non-significant result with a wide CI is uninformative — you just don't have enough data.

If the CI is wide, I'd check retrospective power: were we underpowered given the variance we actually observed? If yes, the right call is usually to extend the test or increase sample size, not to conclude no effect. If the CI is narrow and the upper bound is below our pre-specified MDE, I'm confident enough to call it a null result and move on.

What I avoid is a reflexive "run it longer" without checking power — that can just turn a high variance result into a significant but meaningless one at scale. And I avoid subgroup fishing at this stage — if you didn't pre-specify a subgroup analysis, a significant subgroup result post-hoc is usually noise.먼저 p-value가 아닌 신뢰구간(CI)을 봅니다. 의미 있는 효과 크기를 제외하는 좁은 CI의 비유의미한 결과는 실제로 유용한 정보입니다 — 효과가 작다고 합리적 신뢰도로 말할 수 있습니다. 넓은 CI의 비유의미한 결과는 정보가 없는 것 — 단지 데이터가 부족한 것입니다.

CI가 넓다면 사후 검정력 확인을 합니다: 실제 관찰된 분산을 고려했을 때 검정력이 부족했나요? 그렇다면 보통 효과 없음이 아니라 표본 크기를 늘리거나 기간을 연장하는 것이 맞습니다. CI가 좁고 상한이 사전에 정한 MDE 이하라면, 귀무 결과로 결론 짓고 다음으로 넘어갑니다.

피하는 것: 검정력 확인 없이 반사적으로 "더 오래 실행" — 그건 높은 분산 결과를 규모에서 유의미하지만 무의미한 것으로 만들 수 있습니다. 그리고 이 시점에서 사후 서브그룹 탐색 — 사전에 명시하지 않은 서브그룹 분석의 유의미한 결과는 대부분 노이즈입니다.
⚑ Strong Hire — CI-first interpretation, retrospective power check, narrow CI as positive evidence of no effect, explicit warning against subgroup fishing. A Hire answer says "check if we're underpowered, then consider running longer."⚑ Strong Hire — CI 우선 해석, 사후 검정력 확인, 효과 없음의 양의 증거로서의 좁은 CI, 서브그룹 탐색에 대한 명시적 경고. Hire 수준 답변: "검정력 부족인지 확인하고, 더 오래 실행을 고려한다."

Interviewer Tips — Conducting in English as a Non-Native Speaker

Practical prep for Haewon
Before the Interview
What to prepareWhy it helps
Read Section 1 script aloud Rehearsing the opening reduces first-minute nerves. You don't need to memorise it — just be comfortable with the flow. Knowing exactly how you'll introduce yourself frees mental space for listening to his answers.
Say the follow-up questions out loud Questions that read naturally sometimes feel awkward when spoken. Read each section's follow-ups aloud once. Adjust any phrase that feels unnatural in your voice — you can simplify it.
Prepare 3–4 short transition phrases Having ready-made bridges reduces cognitive load in real time. Examples below.
Don't over-explain your English You don't need to apologise or mention language at all. Speak with normal confidence — the candidate is focused on answering well, not evaluating your English.
Transition Phrases to Have Ready
Moving forward
  • "That's helpful, thank you. Let's move to the next question."
  • "Okay, I have a follow-up on that."
  • "Let me give you a scenario."
  • "One more question on this, then we'll move on."
Asking to clarify / go deeper
  • "Can you say more about that?"
  • "Can you give me a concrete example?"
  • "Just to make sure I understand — are you saying that…?"
  • "What happened next?"
If you missed something she said
  • "Sorry, could you repeat that? I want to make sure I follow."
  • "Can you walk me through that part again?"
  • (Asking to repeat sounds like careful listening — not a language issue.)
Time management
  • "I want to make sure we cover everything, so let me move on."
  • "Let's keep going — we have a few more sections."
  • "We're coming up on time, so let me ask one more question."
During the Interview
SituationWhat to do
He gives a long answer and you lose track It's fine to say "Let me just check my notes for a moment" — this is normal interviewer behaviour and makes you look organised, not confused. Pause, find your place, continue.
He uses a term you don't recognise Ask: "I want to make sure I'm following — what do you mean by [term]?" This is actually a good interviewer behaviour: you're testing whether he can explain technical concepts clearly, which is part of the job.
He gives a short or vague answer Don't rush to the next question. Just say: "Can you tell me more about that?" or simply wait — silence after a question is normal. Candidates often add more when given a moment.
You need to interrupt to stay on time "That's really interesting — I want to make sure we cover the next part, so let me move us forward." You can always say "I'd love to come back to that if we have time at the end."
His accent or pace makes it hard to follow Ask once: "Sorry, I missed that last part — could you say it again?" If it's a consistent issue, slow down your own speaking slightly — people naturally mirror pace.
The SQL session gets awkward (silence while he codes) It's normal to be quiet while he thinks. You can say "Take your time" once, then stay quiet. Don't fill silence with hints unless he's clearly stuck for more than 2–3 minutes.
Language Simplification — It's Fine to Rephrase

You don't need to read the questions verbatim from this doc. If a question feels unnatural to say out loud, simplify it. The meaning matters more than the exact phrasing. For example:

"Walk me through the causal assumptions you had to make.""What assumptions did you make to get to that number? And how confident are you really?"
"Distinguish 'no effect' from 'underpowered test.'""How do you know whether the test found no difference — or just wasn't big enough to detect one?"
