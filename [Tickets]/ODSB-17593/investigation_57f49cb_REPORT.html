1. KB AIRBRIDGE SoI (Share of Installs) — The Regime Shift

Metric definition. What this chart plots is Share of Installs (SoI) = Moloco-attributed KB install postbacks / total KB install postbacks received from AIRBRIDGE. The denominator includes both Moloco-attributed postbacks AND cross-network postbacks that AIRBRIDGE forwards to Moloco (because Moloco had prior click/impression participation). This is an SoI-style metric, not a raw attribution-engine health metric.

Why spend overlay matters. A drop in SoI is intrinsically confounded with KB's Moloco spend level — lower spend → Moloco wins fewer auctions → fewer attribution-eligible clicks → lower SoI mechanically. To distinguish infrastructure-break from spend-driven mechanical SoI decline, we overlay daily KB iOS spend (USD). The decisive test: if spend recovers but SoI stays flat, the driver is infrastructure, not budget.

KB's SoI is a real regime shift, not a chronic state, and the spend overlay confirms it is not primarily spend-driven:

  • Feb baseline: SoI 10-14% at ~$130/day spend — stable.
  • Mar 1-5 zero episode: spend = $0 (PIM-2841 action-model outage blackout) → SoI near zero is partially mechanical (no spend, no Moloco attributions) compounded by the action-model outage where KB was on the impacted-campaigns sheet.
  • Mar 6-20 recovery: spend rose to $150-360/day; SoI tracked with it at 7-14%.
  • Mar 14-15 dip: spend collapsed (Mar 14 = $34), so SoI zero-episode is largely mechanical — not a second action-model outage in itself.
  • Mar 19-20 REBOUND to 10-14%: spend was at $250-360/day. Pre-cliff peak.
  • Mar 21-22 cliff: spend dipped briefly (Mar 21 = $34) but even on Mar 22 spend recovered to $128 — yet SoI stayed at 0.78%. First signal that SoI is decoupled from spend.
  • Mar 23-27 decisive divergence: spend surged to $272-366/day (above Feb baseline), yet SoI only reached 6.4% peak and faded to 3.5%. Spend recovered, SoI did not.
  • Apr 2-16 sustained non-recovery: spend consistently $200-304/day (well above Feb's $130 baseline) while SoI sits at 0.8-1.9%. This is the clearest evidence that the post-Mar-20 SoI collapse is an infrastructure break, not spend-driven.
Source: A1, A6, A11, BQ-32d. Spend = fact_dsp_core (daily KB iOS, USD). SoI = pb-level Moloco-attributed / total-forwarded. Feb attribution % values for unsampled days use weekly-average interpolation from A6 where daily values were not surfaced.
Decisive test passed: If the driver were "Moloco spend too low → fewer attributions," we would see SoI track spend. Instead, Apr 3-16 spend was +90% above Feb baseline while SoI stayed at ~12% of baseline. The two curves visibly decouple at Mar 21 and never re-couple. This is the infrastructure-break signature.

Key insight from the Mar 31-Apr 1 spike (Event C): The spike is volume-only. AIRBRIDGE sent ~7× more postbacks in the onset hour (Mar 31 15:00 UTC = Apr 1 00:00 KST, exactly midnight KST), but attribution rate stayed at 0.4-3.9% throughout — essentially the same as the post-cliff baseline. This means AIRBRIDGE briefly resumed forwarding more install postbacks to Moloco, but these postbacks arrived with the same attribution-breaking condition (empty campaign_id / broken macro substitution) as the post-cliff baseline. The spike further reinforces the MMP-forwarding-layer root cause hypothesis: there's intermittent upstream activity on the AIRBRIDGE side, but it is not accompanied by a fix of whatever is preventing Moloco from crediting those postbacks. The spike onset aligning with midnight KST to the hour is consistent with a scheduled/batch-driven event on the MMP or publisher side, not a Moloco-internal platform change.
Why this all matters for the original Mar 20 cliff narrative: The pb table counts all postbacks AIRBRIDGE sends to Moloco for the bundle — both Moloco-attributed AND cross-network. A uniform -71%/-73% drop across both buckets on Mar 20 means AIRBRIDGE is sending 71% fewer postbacks to Moloco for KB — this is at the MMP→Moloco forwarding layer, upstream of Moloco's attribution engine entirely. Any Moloco-attribution-side code change (MM-138, PR #70212, etc.) cannot produce this signal. Combined with Event 8's composition (volume-only recovery with attribution still broken), the picture is consistent with: (1) Mar 20 onset of AIRBRIDGE-side forwarding disruption + upstream tagging/macro issue; (2) intermittent volume fluctuations post-cliff (including the Mar 31 – Apr 1 spike); (3) attribution rate staying low throughout because the tagging/macro issue persists. Points decisively to KB app-side / DPLAN360 agency / AIRBRIDGE tenant configuration as the surface to investigate.

5. Apr 3 Feedback Loop — Creative & Exchange Mix Shift

The daily cr_format and exchange-mix trajectories (Feb 1 – Apr 18) materially refine the feedback-loop narrative. The prior 3-snapshot view (Pre / Post / Recent) implied a gradual climb; the daily view reveals the shift is actually a step-function on Apr 3, triggered by the budget cut — not by the Mar 20 cliff. Two separate regime changes exist and should not be conflated.

Finding 1 — nl explosion is a step-function on Apr 3 (not gradual from Mar 21)

  • Feb baseline: nl share 4-12%, stable; ib dominant (~65-75%).
  • Mar 21 – Apr 2 (post-cliff, pre-budget-cut): nl still 2-4% — essentially unchanged from pre-cliff. The Mar 20 cliff did NOT trigger any cr_format shift.
  • Apr 3: +27.9 pp in one day (3.47% → 32.0% nl share).
  • Apr 4: 45.3%. Apr 9: 60%. Plateau 58-62% through Apr 18.
  • Thresholds crossed: 20% on Apr 3, 40% on Apr 4, 60% on Apr 9 — textbook regime change in 7 days.
Source: fact_dsp_creative daily cr_format impression share, Feb 1 – Apr 18. Stacked area: ib (dominant in Feb baseline) / ni / nl (the step-change) / ii. Apr 3 discontinuity is sharp and obvious.

Finding 2 — nl explosion is entirely a KAKAO phenomenon (complete publisher migration)

  • Pre-Apr 3: ~99% of nl impressions were on INMOBI (Feb 1: 99%).
  • Post-Apr 3: 94-98% of nl impressions on KAKAO, INMOBI drops to 2-6%, all others ≈ 0.
  • This is a complete publisher migration of nl inventory — INMOBI → KAKAO — concurrent with the budget cut. Not just a format shift, but a publisher-plus-format shift.

Finding 3 — Exchange-mix shift happened SEPARATELY, earlier (around the Mar 20 cliff)

  • KAKAO share progression: Feb 15: 28% → Mar 20: 76% → Apr 3: 76% → Apr 10: 83%.
  • KAKAO consolidation was already underway before Apr 3 — this happened around the Mar 20 window, independently of the cr_format step-change.
  • What Apr 3 changed was what format KAKAO serves, not whether KAKAO dominates. After Apr 3 the model shifts KAKAO-served inventory from mixed (ib/ni) to nl-heavy.
